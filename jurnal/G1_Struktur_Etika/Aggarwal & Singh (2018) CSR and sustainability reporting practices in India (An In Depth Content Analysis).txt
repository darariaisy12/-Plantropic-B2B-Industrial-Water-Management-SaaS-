R. Bowen – considered the pioneer of CSR – defined CSR as “the obligations of
businessmen to pursue those policies, to make those decisions, or to follow those lines
of action which are desirable in terms of objectives and values of our society” (Bowen,
1953, p. 6).

                                           Theoretical background
                                           The drivers of SR include firm-size, profitability, age, media-exposure, ownership structure
                                           and capital structure (Dienes et al., 2016). The probable motives of companies behind
                                           undertaking SR are discussed below.


                                           Regulatory pressure
                                           In some form, CSR/SR has entered into the realm of mandatory requirements in several
                                           countries. The G20 members – European Union and 19 countries including India, China and
                                           the UK and USA– require environmental, social and governance (ESG) disclosures. Such
                                           regulations impel companies to undertake SR to avoid disciplinary actions. This can be
                                           linked to the legal dimension of CSR, wherein organizations are expected to meet only
                                           minimum legal requirements (Carroll, 1991). Thus, if a company makes meager disclosures
                                           only to comply with the SEBI BRR requirement and doesn’t voluntarily disclose information
                                           as per global standards such as GRI; such a company is perhaps reporting merely out of
                                           regulatory pressure.


                                           Legitimacy theory
                                           Legitimacy theory proposes existence of an apparent contract between the company and
                                           society, which compels companies to meet societal norms to ensure their survival (Burhan
                                           and Rahmanti, 2012; Schmelzer, 2013). CSR/SR lends legitimacy/acceptability to corporate
                                           actions by influencing the public perception affirmatively and helps in avoiding undesirable
                                           media coverage (Patten, 1992; Deegan, 2002; Campbell et al., 2003). Thus, legitimacy



PAGE 1034   j SOCIAL RESPONSIBILITY JOURNAL j VOL. 15 NO. 8 2019
theory propagates the ethical dimension of CSR, wherein firms are expected to comply with
social norms beyond legal requirements (Carroll, 1991). Thus, if a company makes
considerably more society/community disclosures over other dimensions, it is possibly
driven by legitimacy theory.


Stakeholder theory
Stakeholder theory advocates that organizations should pursue the interests of all
stakeholder groups, including investors, customers, suppliers, employees, government,
community and environment in an equitable manner (Clarkson, 1995). Stakeholders are
capable of affecting the organization’s performance and reputation. SR helps firms in
building strong ties with stakeholders yielding several benefits such as lower risk, enhanced
reputation and competitive advantage (Barnett, 2007; Schmelzer, 2013). Stakeholder theory
corresponds to the discretionary dimension of CSR, which urges organizations to be good
corporate citizens who contribute back to the society while going beyond social norms and
laws (Carroll, 1991). Therefore, the companies that voluntarily disclose ESG information with
quantitative data while catering to stakeholders across-the-board are adopting the
stakeholder theory perspective.


Literature review and hypotheses development
CSR research gained popularity in the 1980s. Initially, CSR studies were conducted only in
developed countries such as the USA and UK. Several previous researchers have
investigated the determinants of CSR/sustainability disclosures in terms of firm-specific
factors such as firm-size, profitability, industry and ownership structure (Jain and Winner,
2016; Mertens et al., 2012).


Corporate social responsibility/sustainability reporting in India
CSR has become a buzzword for India Inc. in recent years, particularly after the enactment
of the New Companies Act, 2013, which requires companies to spend at least 2 per cent of
their average profits on CSR activities. CSR/SR in India commenced in the 1980s with the
mandatory disclosure on the Conservation of Energy in Directors’ Report. Over the years,
India has progressed radically in the sphere of CSR. In 2012, the SEBI made it mandatory
for the top 100 listed companies to practice the National Voluntary Guidelines (NVG) on
social, environmental and economic responsibility issued by the Ministry of Corporate
Affairs (MCA) and to uniformly disclose their CSR activities in BRR as part of the Annual
Report (SEBI, 2012, August 13). From April 1, 2016 onward, the BRR requirement has been
extended to the top 500 listed companies. SEBI has also clarified that failure to provide BRR
shall be interpreted as non-compliance with Clause 55 of the listing agreement calling for
grave aftermaths such as heavy penalties, fines, suspensions and even delisting if non-
compliance persists. Recently, the SEBI recommended and urged the top 500 listed
companies to embrace integrated reporting on a voluntary basis from financial year (FY)
2017-18. Figure 1 illustrates the progression of CSR/sustainability in India.
Only a few studies have investigated CSR/SR practices in India, especially after the
aforesaid regulatory developments. Some notable Indian studies in recent times are Laskar
and Maji (2016); and Jain and Winner (2016). The previous studies largely analyzed CSR
disclosures in terms of presence/absence in corporate reports while ignoring the quality
aspect. Also, social and environmental dimensions alone have been emphasized hitherto,
while there is a dearth of research on other dimensions such as human rights and
governance (Murray et al., 2006; Bayoud et al., 2012; Khaveh et al., 2012). Moreover,
previous studies predominantly assessed disclosures based on the GRI guidelines and
overlooked the Indian scenario. The current study seeks to bridge this gap.

                                           Sustainability reporting quantity and quality
                                           Previous studies have mostly used binary scale (0-1; Yang et al., 2010; Kapoor and

                                           evaluate CSR disclosures (Yi and Yu, 2010; Guidry and Patten, 2010; Ameer and Othman,

                                                   companies.

Sustainability reporting and industry-type
Industry is another important variable that influences SR as different industries are subject
to diverse social–environmental concerns. The companies belonging to environmentally
sensitive industries (ESI) such as oil and gas and metals/mining are generally subjected to
more stakeholder pressure than others (Neu et al., 1998; Guidry and Patten, 2010; Reverte,
2012). Stakeholders, especially investors, pay more attention toward CSR performance of
ESI companies, demanding more CSR information (Choi, 1999; Frost and Wilmshurst, 2000;
Young and Marais, 2012). Consequently, ESI companies make more CSR disclosures to
improve stakeholders’ perceptions (Patten, 1992; Pled and Latridis, 2012). Moreover, a few
studies have concluded that there exists a more evident positive association between CSR
disclosure quality and CFP of ESI firms (Plumlee et al., 2008; Reverte, 2012). Thus, it is
critical to investigate the role of industry-type in general and ESI, in particular, in influencing
SR practices in India. Consequently, the third and fourth hypotheses are as follows:
   H3.    Sustainability reporting (SR) practices differ by industry type.
   H3a. SR quantity differs by industry type.
   H3b. SR quality differs by industry type.
   H4.    SR practices of ESI companies are significantly different from non-ESI companies.
   H4a. SR quantity of ESI companies is significantly different from non-ESI companies.
   H4b. SR quality of ESI companies is significantly different from non-ESI companies.


Sustainability reporting and ownership structure
The study further attempts to gain deeper insights into SR practices in India on the basis of
ownership structure, i.e. public vis-à-vis private sectors. Public sector enterprises are those
in which Government has a major shareholding, while private sector companies have no
Government stake. There are contrasting views about the importance of CSR in public and
private sector organizations. One school of thought upholds that private sector companies
are allegedly less CSR-oriented as they are highly profit-oriented, unlike public-sector
undertakings (PSUs), which are governed by social objectives. Jain and Winner (2016) also
concluded that public-sector companies make better CSR disclosures than private
companies. In contrast, Sahu and Panigraphy (2016) observed that companies in the
private sector perform better in CSR. To review this issue, the following fifth hypothesis is
developed:
   H5.    SR practices differ by ownership structure.
   H5a. SR quantity differs by ownership structure.
   H5b. SR quality differs by ownership structure.


Sustainability reporting and economic performance
Several researchers in past have examined the linkage between SR and CFP (Buys et al.,
2011; Burhan and Rahmanti, 2012). According to slack resources view, financially
prosperous companies have more surplus resources and hence expected to be more
committed toward CSR (Udayasankar, 2008; Palmer, 2012). According to political cost
perspective, big companies are more exposed to media, public and socio-regulatory
pressure and hence are more likely to communicate their CSR activities to gain legitimacy
(Cormier and Magnan, 2003; Setyorini and Ishak, 2012). Drawing on aforesaid arguments,

Content analysis process
Content analysis is a qualitative research technique used to interpret and draw inferences in
an objective/systematic and quantifiable manner by evaluating textual material such as
reports against predetermined criteria. It has been extensively used in previous CSR
communication research (Abbott and Monsen, 1979; Morhardt et al., 2002; Daub, 2007;
Vormedal and Ruud, 2009).
First, we developed a comprehensive Sustainability Reporting Index (SRI) based on several
standards and guidelines, particularly bearing in mind the Indian scenario, viz. GRI G4
guidelines, UNGC Principles, ISO 26000, SA 8000, NVG by MCA, SEBI–BRR Framework
and Companies Act, 2013. The 80-item equally weighted index comprises 7 dimensions,
namely, economic (8 items), governance and ethics (15), environment (16), community (9),
customers and product responsibility (8), employees and labor practices (17) and human
rights (7), as presented in Figure 3. See “Appendix 1” for the complete index.
Subsequently, coding has been done by authors from a qualitative and quantitative
perspective. To examine SR quantity, the presence of each item is checked in the corporate
reports and website of each company and a score of 0 (absence) or 1 (presence) is
assigned accordingly. The disclosures are further assessed in terms of quality using a six-
point (0-5) scale, which has been used for the first time for evaluating CSR/sustainability
disclosures. The scale has been developed based on previous studies – Daub (2007),
Vormedal and Ruud (2009) – and is described in Table I. The scores so obtained are then
expressed in percentage, wherein higher scores reflect the better ability of the company to
communicate with stakeholders transparently.
Besides, a third-party/independent researcher also performed the content analysis of
reports for a sub-sample representing around 10 per cent of the whole sample. Inter-coder
reliability (ICR) estimation is critical for establishing the trustworthiness of data generated

                                           A variety of quantitative techniques were used to test the research hypotheses. Descriptive

                                           Sustainability reporting practices in India
                                           Results revealed that approximately one-third (36.67 per cent) sample companies publish
                                           standalone CSR/SR while a mere 28.33 per cent companies follow GRI guidelines. Around
                                           one-fourth companies issue both BRR and GRI reports, and only 23.33 per cent companies
                                           publish externally assured GRI report. These statistics demonstrate sparseness of
                                           sustainability reporting in India. As the adoption of the GRI framework is limited even among
                                           the top-notch companies, regulatory pressure seems to be the major motive behind
                                           sustainability reporting in India.
                                           Wipro Ltd. is the only company to have voluntarily sought external-assurance of BRR.
                                           Besides, all companies have prepared BRR, except Marico Industries Ltd. and Coal India
                                           Ltd.; however, they have published a separate sustainability report. SEBI allows companies
                                           not to prepare BRR if BRR principles are mapped with sustainability disclosures. Further

                                           mapping, however, Marico had not followed any international standard; moreover, mapping

Quantity and quality of sustainability reporting
First, descriptive statistics, as reported in Table III, indicate that average reporting is done
on three-fourth items that too of mediocre quality. Further, the majority of companies have
“Below Average” SR quality, 25 per cent have “Above Average” quality, and none of the
companies has excellent quality, as is shown in Figure 4.
Further, it is found that there is a significant difference between the SR quality and quantity
scores (p = 0.00). Moreover, the effect size is large, exceeding 0.8 (Cohen’s d = 1.9),
implying that SR quality is substantially lower than SR quantity, providing support for H1.
However, the difference between rankings on the basis of the aforesaid scores is not
statistically significant (p = 0.88), as can be seen in Table III. This is probably owing to a
high positive correlation between SR quality and SR quantity (r = 0.941; p = 0.00)

Conclusions and discussion
This is a leading study providing valuable insights into SR practices in India, post the
mandatory BRR requirement. We developed an all-inclusive 80-item index based on several
national and international standards for assessing the SR performance of companies.
Content analysis reliability is duly established using Krippendorff’s alpha test. Of the seven
hypotheses, findings support H1, H2, H3, H4 and H6 while not supporting H5 and H7. It is
observed that only one-third top-listed companies in India publish standalone CSR/SR, with
a major bulk having a “below-average” quality.
GAIL is adjudged as the best company, while Sun TV Network secured the last rank.
Although SR quantity and quality crudely provide similar rankings, the SR-quality score
is substantially lower than the SR-quantity score. Besides, only one-fourth companies
issue externally assured sustainability reports. This raises concerns on the reliability of
such disclosures. Thus, companies are advised to publish externally verified
sustainability reports containing specific and detailed information with quantitative data
to enhance transparency and trustworthiness bringing SR at par with financial
reporting.
Further, huge differences are observed in the quantity and quality of CSR disclosures
against diverse dimensions. Community-based disclosures are mostly emphasized upon by
Indian companies, while employee-related items are the least reported. Thus, companies in
India are perhaps driven by legitimacy theory approach as community/society is more
centered upon than other stakeholders in their haste to gain acceptance from public and
society at large, and to augment corporate reputation.
The study also discovered significant differences in SR practices by industry-type with energy
sector as the best performer. Also, environmentally sensitive companies are observed making
more CSR/sustainability disclosures plausibly to improve their rapport with stakeholders. This
finding is consistent with Patten (1992), Liu and Anbumozhi (2009). Telecom companies,
being the worst performers, need to toil hard to improve their CSR/sustainability

                                           Implications, recommendations and scope for further research
                                           The present study has several implications for corporates, practitioners, policymakers and
                                           stakeholders. Low ranking companies need to radically mend their CSR/sustainability
                                           communication strategies to meet regulatory and stakeholder demands. Besides, our
                                           findings suggest that currently companies in India are driven by regulatory pressure and
                                           legitimacy approach to SR. The companies should rather follow stakeholder theory
                                           addressing the needs of all stakeholders equitably by making appropriate disclosures on all
                                           dimensions, especially with employee-related disclosures being the worst of all. Also,
                                           suitable amendments should be made in GRI guidelines and SEBI–BRR framework to avoid
                                           patchy responses in the form of yes/no and to encourage specific quantifiable disclosures
                                           by companies. Also, a lack of uniformity in SR formats and means adopted by companies
                                           calls for a universal standard on SR on similar lines as International Financial Reporting
                                           Standards (IFRS), together with mandatory external-assurance, to bring uniformity and
                                           reliability in sustainability reporting (Mertens et al., 2012).
                                           It is worth mentioning here that the results of the present study should be interpreted with
                                           caution, as it is purely based on self-reported disclosures made by companies in the public
                                           domain. They may/may not convey the real performance of the company. The scope of the
                                           present study was limited to assessing the frequency and quality of CSR/sustainability
                                           disclosures; however, determining their authenticity was beyond the scope of this study.
                                           Future researchers should investigate the relationship of SR with profitability and other CFP
                                           indicators by performing a longitudinal study with a larger sample of top 500 listed
                                           companies. There is also a need to explore integrated reporting, a path ahead of SR, which
                                           is still a nascent phenomenon in India. Furthermore, a cross-cultural study across several
                                           countries may provide vital acumens on CSR/SR issues.
