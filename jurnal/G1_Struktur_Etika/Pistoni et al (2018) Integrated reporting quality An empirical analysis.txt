           ABSTRACT
           Integrated Reporting (IR) represents the most recent evolution of the corporate reporting move-
           ment. Despite the proposed beneﬁts to stakeholders and the number of contributes aimed at
           identifying best practices in its adoption, IR is still scarcely diffused among companies and when
           it is adopted the framework is not fully implemented. Starting from these premises, this paper
           aims to assess the quality of integrated reports issued by ﬁrms. We have developed a scoring
           model and an IR Scoreboard (IRS), which were applied to analyze 116 integrated reports issued
           in years 2013 and 2014. The main ﬁndings show that IR quality is low. Generally, ﬁrms follow
           the IR framework, but scarce information is disclosed on aspects such as capital, the business
           model, strategic priorities, and the value creation process; more attention is given to the IR form
           than to its content. Copyright © 2018 John Wiley & Sons, Ltd and ERP Environment

practices in its adoption, recently some studies have showed that IR is still scarcely diffused among companies
and when it is adopted the framework is not fully implemented (de Villiers et al., 2014; Global Reporting Initiative
(GRI), 2013a). A recent study (International Integrated Reporting Council (IIRC), 2014) showed great interest in IR,
even though the majority of the interviewed companies declared a reluctance and difﬁculties in fully implementing
the IR framework. These mainly concerned the request of too much conﬁdential information, technical issues in

framework, but scarce information is disclosed on relevant aspects such as capital, the business model, strategic
priorities, and the value creation process. A limited depth of analysis characterizes all sections of the integrated
report. More attention is given to the form than to the content of IR.

on the quality assessment of voluntary disclosure. The second section presents the research framework and
methodology. The third section highlights and discusses the empirical ﬁndings. Finally, the last section reports
some ﬁnal remarks.



Literature Review
According to the IIRC (2013a), an Integrated Report is a ‘concise communication about how an organization’s
strategy, governance, performance and prospects, in the context of its external environment, lead to the creation
of value over the short, medium and long term’. IR provides ‘the holistic and integrated representation of the ﬁrm’s
status according to the three performance perspectives: ﬁnancial, social and environmental’. IR is much more than
bringing together ﬁnancial and sustainability reporting, as it provides comprehensive and comprehensible informa-
tion about the organization’s total performance, prospective as well as retrospective. IR deﬁnes also a systemic
representation of the ﬁrm’s performance, showing, evaluating, and controlling all the potential risks – ﬁnancial
and non-ﬁnancial – that could damage the ﬁrm’s activities (Bebbington and Larrinaga, 2014). The IR framework
is based on a wide concept of integration (Pistoni and Songini, 2015):
• the company performance is interpreted as the result of the combined use of different kinds of capital provided by
  different stakeholders: ﬁnancial, human, technical, intellectual, social, and environmental capital;
• the framework requires to make explicit the relation between the ﬁrm’s strategy and performance;
• the integration is also pursued through organizational processes and procedures;
• the framework promotes integrated thinking and a strong collaboration among functions in order to improve the
  ﬁrm’s value creation in the long run;
• the value created is not only a result of the ﬁrm’s internal activities but also of the quality of the relationships with
  different stakeholders.
   Previous research on IR has focused on the analysis of IR’s role and objectives (Beattie and Smith, 2013; Beck
et al., 2017; Brown and Dillard, 2014; Dumay et al., 2016; Lodhia, 2014; Stacchezzini et al., 2016), the IR
framework’s main issues (Abeysekera, 2013; Haller and van Staden, 2014; Higgins et al., 2014), and on the
relations between IR and CSR and sustainability disclosure (Adams et al., 2016; Maas et al., 2016; Martinez-
Ferrero et al., 2015; Sierra-García et al., 2015).
   Recently, the literature has highlighted the determinants of IR focusing on why companies are producing inte-
grated reports, and what are the explanatory factors for their adoption (Frías-Aceituno et al., 2013; Jensen and Berg,

by case study analysis (Burke and Clark, 2016; Havlová, 2015; Mio, 2016). Some authors have identiﬁed open ques-

   Conversely, the literature on IR quality assessment is quite limited (Marx and Mohammadali-Haji, 2014; Ruiz-
Lozano and Tirado-Valencia, 2016). In 2012, Ernst & Young launched the Excellence in Integrated Report Awards,
aimed at analyzing the state of the art of IR in South Africa and at promoting better quality of IR and best practices
in its adoption. In 2014, the IIRC itself published a document on ‘Assurance on IR: an exploration of issues’, with
the aim of generating interest on quality issues. The Financial Reporting Council (2014) delivered ‘Guidance on the
Strategic Report’, which focused on the quality of corporate reporting, in terms of its capacity to present the strategic
elements that determine ﬁrm performance and value creation. Eccles and Krzus (2014, p. 191) stated that: ‘it is not
solely the absolute number of companies practicing integrated reporting, but the quality of adoption that matters …
Although companies may achieve a truly integrated report by other means, the effectiveness with which they apply
these frameworks and standards will determine how useful these reports are to investors’.
   The main critical issues on the quality of IR highlighted by the literature are: the absence of connectivity among
strategy, the business model, performance and future outlook, due to the poor narrative ﬂow and the limited use of
diagrams and maps; the presence of an informative gap in areas such as governance, stakeholder engagement, and
the materiality process; the inadequate description of the business model; and internal auditing, completeness of
information, and limited third-party veriﬁcation (Eccles and Serafeim, 2015).
   In addition, some studies outlined that even though companies declare the application of the IR framework, they
then implement it only partially. All such evidence has led the interest of practitioners, managers, and academics to
shift from the type and quantity of information included in integrated reports towards their quality. In particular,
quality refers to the capacity of IR to present the strategic elements that describe ﬁrm performance and value crea-
tion. It is ‘a dimension linked to multiple characteristics and information areas that should be analyzed together to
correctly appreciate its range’ (Garegnani et al., 2015, p. 542). ‘Quality reporting is not about religiously complying
with a framework, but about approaching strategic reporting with the right mindset. It’s about taking a longer-term,
broader, more operational perspective that will challenge how companies think, operate, monitor and report
performance in a connected way’ (PricewaterhouseCoopers, 2014).
   Hence, quality assessment is not related only to the quantity of information reported. Some authors suggest that
corporate disclosure should be informative and signiﬁcant, understandable, comparable, comprehensive, material,
reliable, and easily available (Garegnani et al., 2015). According to Unerman (2000), to assess the quality of
disclosure it is necessary to consider its contents as a whole. Other characteristics to be analyzed include disclosure’s
themes, range of issues and styles, nature, scope, coverage, time period, and location (Asif et al., 2013; Hackston and
Milne, 1996).
   A number of guidelines have been proposed to assess the quality of CSR and sustainability voluntary disclosure.
According to Hammond and Miles (2004), three main forms of assessment approaches exist: (1) formal monitoring
procedures (professional scoring protocols, ranking exercises); (2) award schemes, aimed to highlight and reward
best practices; and (3) academic quality assessment, which checks quality in different areas of disclosure, mainly
using content analysis.
   However, previous literature did not reach a consensus on which items to evaluate when assessing the quality of
voluntary disclosure. The main reason can be traced in the difﬁculty to clearly and univocally deﬁne the concept of
quality of disclosure. ‘When a study covers voluntary disclosure, there is no predeﬁned list of items, so it is
necessary to deﬁne a list that will be applied to all of the companies surveyed’ (Garegnani et al., 2015, p. 546). So,
usually the list of items is identiﬁed considering the different contributions of previous literature and regulatory
frameworks (Adhikari and Tondkar, 1992).
   With regard to the reasons explaining the quality of voluntary disclosure, three main theories can be taken into
consideration. Namely, these theories give insight about the reasons underlying the low quality of such
disclosure. First, the proprietary cost theory proposes that ﬁrms limit voluntary disclosure due to the existence of
some disclosure costs including: data and information measurement, elaboration and communication, and the
diffusion of strategic information which can give competitors an advantage (Dye, 1986; Verrecchia, 1983, 1990;
Wagenhofer, 1990). Second, as highlighted by positive accounting theory and particularly in the light of the political

Empirical Analysis
Research Objectives and the Quality Assessment Framework
In light of the literature on critical issues in IR adoption, and on the quality of voluntary disclosure (Eccles and
Serafeim, 2015; Stubbs and Higgins, 2014), and considering the main theories concerning the ﬁrms’ willingness
to engage in voluntary disclosure, this paper aims to assess the quality of integrated reports, and positions itself
in the ‘academic quality assessment’ stream of research (Hammond and Miles, 2004).
   In particular, we posit two research questions (RQ 1 and RQ 2) and two hypotheses (HP 1 and HP 2):
RQ 1: What is the quality of integrated reports?

HP 1: Integrated Reports issued by companies show a low quality

RQ 2: Has the quality of IR improved during (over) time?

HP 2: Over time IR quality improves but only incrementally
   In order to answer these questions and to test the hypotheses, we have ﬁrstly developed a framework for quality
assessment of integrated reports. To deﬁne the list of items and build a scoring model, we have referred to the 11
attributes of the quality of CSR disclosure proposed by Hammond and Miles (2004): Quantitative disclosure,
Third-party veriﬁcation, Establishment of, and reporting against, (appropriate) targets, ‘Warts and all’ reporting,
Adoption of reporting guidelines and standards, Ability to accurately assess performance from disclosure, Clear
statement of vision from the CEO, Good coverage of signiﬁcant issues, Wide access of the document, Reporting
of normalized data, and Achievement of awards/accolades. Even though these attributes have been identiﬁed in
the speciﬁc context of CSR disclosure, Hammond and Miles’ framework includes not only the assessment of
non-ﬁnancial and qualitative information but also of ﬁnancial and quantitative information, as the authors explain
(p. 75): ‘The CSR literature indicates that disclosure, which is quantitative and/or ﬁnancial, ‘warts and all’ and com-
prehensive with respect to coverage would be classiﬁed as of a higher quality than discursive, self-laudatory, selected
disclosure’. Moreover, the attributes of Hammond and Miles (2004) may be considered as broad guidelines suitable
for the evaluation of the quality of both ﬁnancial and non-ﬁnancial (integrated) disclosure. Hence, we have linked
the 11 attributes to four disclosure areas, articulated in 23 variables, which as a whole identify our IRS (Figure 1;
Table 1):
• Background area evaluates whether the document presents an introduction devoted to discussing issues, such as:
  (1) motivations underlying the choice of adopting IR; (2) objectives pursued by IR; (3) beneﬁciaries of the docu-
  ment; (4) the manager in charge of the IR process; (5) the CEO’s commitment; (6) the title of the report; and
  (7) consistency of IR with generally applied disclosure standards. Such aspects have been identiﬁed following
  the recommendations of the IR framework (IIRC, 2013b), as well as previous studies (Garegnani et al., 2015).
• Assurance and reliability area assesses whether (1) an internal audit and/or (2) a third-party veriﬁcation has been
  carried out; and (3) the company has received acknowledgements and awards for IR.
• Content area assesses the consistency of the document with the prescriptions of the IR framework regarding eight
  elements (organizational overview and external environment, business model, risks and opportunities, strategy
  and resource allocation, governance, performance, outlook, and basis of presentation) and two fundamental
  concepts (Capital and Value creation process).

    The 23 variables have been grouped in the four areas, trying to achieve the highest possible level of consistency
with the literature on disclosure quality assessment (Botosan, 1997; Garegnani et al., 2015), and cross-referencing
the variables to the IR Guiding Principles of: Stakeholder Relationships (Background area); Reliability and Com-
pleteness (Assurance and Reliability area); Strategic Focus and Future Orientation, Connectivity of Information,
and Materiality (Content area); and Conciseness, and Consistency and Comparability (Form area).
    At the end of the process, we point out that some of the elements in our framework overlap with items previously
proposed by the literature on the quality of ﬁnancial disclosure (Botosan, 1997, p. 331) and codes of ethics (Garegnani
et al., 2015). For example, organizational overview and external environment, business model, strategy and resource al-
location, performance, outlook, and value creation partly overlap with the major elements of Botosan’s DSCORE1: Back-
ground Information, Ten or Five Year Summary of Historical Results, Key Non-Financial Statistics, Projected
Information, Management Discussion and Analysis. On a different note, motivations underlying the choice of adopting
IR, beneﬁciaries of the document, CEO’s commitment, assurance and reliability items, and form items (readability
and clarity, conciseness, and accessibility) partly overlap with commitment from the top, style, and availability, rela-
tions with stakeholders, and compliance procedures in Garegnani et al.’s (2015) scoring model for Codes of Ethics’
quality assessment.
    The following step was to develop a scoring system to assess each variable comprised in each of the four areas of
the IRS. Quantitative scales have been deﬁned to measure comparative positions and allow further analysis (Milne
and Adler, 1999). Scoring systems have been extensively applied and validated in the ﬁelds of both CSR and
sustainability, with reference to social and environmental reporting (Gray et al., 1996; Romolini et al., 2014),
and in the ﬁeld of accounting, concerning disclosure in ﬁnancial reports (Botosan, 1997).
    Concerning the Background area, the presence or absence of each of the seven proposed variables was evaluated.
A score of 0 was given in the case of the absence of the item, while a score of 1 was given if the item was included in
the IR document. The maximum score for this area is 7.
    Referring to the Assurance and reliability area, a score of 0 was given in the case of the absence of each of the three
items, while a score of 1was assigned in the case of the presence of the item. The maximum score for this area is 3.
    As for the Content area, to each of the ten variables was given a score between 0 (absence) and 5 (very high
quality), according to the classiﬁcation scheme presented in Table 2. Such a classiﬁcation considers how the topic
is presented, whether its description is exhaustive, and whether it refers explicitly to the IR guiding principles
(strategic focus and future orientation, connectivity, stakeholder relationships, materiality, reliability and complete-
ness, consistency and comparability). The maximum score achievable in the Content area is 50.
    To each of the three elements of the Form area was given a score between 0 and 5, according to the classiﬁcation
schemes presented in Tables 3–5. The maximum score achievable in the Form area is 15. An overview of the scoring
model is presented in Figure 2.

Research Methodology
The data were collected and codiﬁed using visual content analysis (Weber, 1990) of all the 116 integrated reports of
the 58 sample companies. Two researchers read carefully all 116 integrated reports and autonomously classiﬁed
them consistently with the proposed scoring system. They were trained on the scoring protocol to assure the
reliability of the analysis (Krippendorf, 1980). For the areas Background, Form, and Assurance and reliability, only
few discrepancies emerged, which were discussed within the research group and resolved. During the process of
analysis, the ten items of the Content area turned out to be the most controversial and seemed to involve more
judgement by the two analysts. Hence, we decided to let the two analysts assign independently a score to each of
the ten items and later controlled the inter-rater agreement (reliability of measurement) between the two
analysts calculating the intra-class correlation coefﬁcients (unreported, available upon request). The average
intra-class correlation coefﬁcient for the ten items is 0.82, ranging from a minimum of 0.74 to a maximum of
0.90, indicating a high degree of consistency between the two analysts. In light of the consistency of the indepen-
dent scores, we considered in our analysis the average of the scores autonomously assigned by the two analysts.
   We also tested if the different items in each area were actually measuring the same object and consequently if
some redundant items should be removed. We used the KR20 test for categorical items (Background and Assurance
and reliability areas) and Cronbach’s alpha test for scale (0–5) variables (items in the Content and Form areas)
(Table 7). The alpha level is never equal to or above 0.7 for all the four areas. This provides evidence that each of
the 23 items contributes incrementally to the assessment of the quality of IR and therefore should be kept in the
scoreboard. An alternative interpretation of this result, though, could be that we did not properly group the items

assurance and reliability area (where we have the lowest alpha level equal to 0.09), it is reasonable to expect that sin-
gle items would not be correlated: if an entity has its report externally audited, there is no reason to expect that it
would be also audited internally or that it should receive an award for the quality of the report. On a different note,
for the Background and Content areas, we may expect that an entity could disclose more diligently with regard to
certain aspects, and less diligently on others (it may be also a deliberate tactic to disclose more on items which
are less sensitive to have an excuse to disclose less on more sensitive ones, for the sake of conciseness). Finally,
for the form area, where there is a (not negligible) alpha level of 0.42, the different elements of readability and
clarity, conciseness, and accessibility not necessarily go together (a report may be very clear but too long, or may
be very concise but obscure), even if they all refer to the form of the report and not the substance.

   We applied a methodology for the analysis of the scores consisting of the following steps:
1 describing the whole sample of 116 reports to get a general view of the advancement – at the time of the study – of
  the quality of IR with reference to the four areas that we identiﬁed;
2 comparing the relative total scores for each area (total average score divided by the maximum score achievable in
  the area), in order to evaluate which areas were best taken care of and which areas were relatively overlooked by
  the preparers of the reports;
3 observing the trend in each area, also considering the single items included in each of them, comparing the average
  scores for year 2013 and year 2014, and testing the statistical signiﬁcance of the differences of the means (with a related
  samples (McNemar, 1947) change test for categorical variables, and with a paired samples t-test for scale variables).




Research Findings

To assess: (1) the quality of integrated reports in the four different areas: Background, Assurance and reliability,
Content, and Form (in order to answer RQ 1 What is the quality of integrated reports? and test HP 1 Integrated
Reports issued by companies show a low quality); and (2) changes in the quality of IR from 2013 to 2014 (in order to
answer RQ 2 Has the quality of IR improved during (over) time? and test HP 2: Over time IR quality improves but
only incrementally), we report, ﬁrstly, ﬁndings referring to the overall sample of 116 reports issued by the 58
companies and, then, we examine the differences between 2013 and 2014.


Analysis of the Whole Sample
Tables 8–10 describe the whole sample of 116 integrated reports. The elements in the Background section
provide evidence of a situation that needs to be improved (Table 8). In particular, reporters paid little attention to
the indication of the recipients (only 24%) and the reasons behind the adoption of IR (only 32%).
   As for Assurance and reliability area, we observed that a large majority of the reports have been audited, internally
(57%) and externally (71%), and that almost half of them (46%) received awards or acknowledgments for
quality (Table 8).
   Also the Content area shows considerable room for improvement: ﬁve items out of ten achieve an average score
higher than or equal to 3 (i.e. they obtain a sufﬁcient score). In detail, overview, risks, strategy, governance, and
performance are on average fairly described in the reports, whilst that is not true for the business model, outlook,
the basis of presentation, capital, and the value creation process (Table 9).

    These results suggest that, in general, the content of integrated reports still needs to be developed: when
examining the speciﬁc issues covered, the richest information concerns items commonly disclosed in ﬁrms’
ﬁnancial reports, while the innovative topics of IR are still in their infancy.
    In the Form area, some items perform fairly well (both readability/clarity and accessibility obtain an average score
higher than 3.5 out of a maximum of 5), while conciseness – one of the novelty of IR – reaches on average only 2.66.
Almost half of the companies (43%) achieve a score of 2 or less (Table 9).
    At a glance, if we observe the total scores for the four areas, in Table 10, we see that the best-attended area is
Form. The average total score for Form is 9.9 out of a maximum score of 15 (66%), followed by Assurance and
reliability with an average of 1.7 out of 3 (57%), Content with an average score of 27.3 out of 50 (55%), and ﬁnally
by Background which registers an average score of 3.1 out of 7 (44%).
    In light of these research ﬁndings, we may say that our ﬁrst hypothesis is veriﬁed, that is, the disclosure quality is
still not satisfactory, especially in some areas. The pre-eminence of Form over substantial aspects such as Content
may be linked – inter alia – to the high heterogeneity of results observed between the single items in the
Content category, and in particular, as suggested above, to the fact that the most innovative parts of the IR
framework still lack a truly effective disclosure.
    We have not computed a total score summing up the total scores of the single areas, because the independence
and heterogeneity of the four areas, and the difference in the measurement scales would make interpretation of
the results unclear. For example, assurance and reliability, a very important aspect for evaluation of the quality
of the reports, having a total maximum score of 3, would be underrepresented in a total score ranging from 0 to
75 made up from the sum of the total scores of the four areas.


Trend Analysis (2013 to 2014)
In this section, we examine the trend of disclosure in reports (from 2013 to 2014) separately for each area.

Background
Table 11 shows the percentage of occurrence of the seven items comprising the Background area in years 2013 and
2014, and the differences between the two years, which in general are not of relevant magnitude and, in any case,
never statistically signiﬁcant (as per a related samples (McNemar, 1947) change test).
    The reasons, the goals, the responsible, and the level of commitment of integrated reports are disclosed in 2014
less frequently than in 2013. In general, the numbers are pretty disappointing, possibly because these items have
been already declared in the past and, thus, the preparers of the reports might have thought that they did not need
to repeat them in successive releases.
    The recipients, the linkage to the IR Framework (Framework Standards), and the mention of IR in the title of the
report, by contrast, appear in 2014 more frequently than in 2013. With reference to the ﬁrst point (recipients), we
may expect that, moving along with a truly ‘integrated’ approach, companies tend to adopt a view explicitly devoted
to the materiality issue and, in doing so, they declare and identify more precisely the recipients of integrated reports.
    The more frequent mention of the Framework and of IR in the title of the report could be related to the time ef-
fect (the Framework was launched in late 2013). In general, for the total score in the Background Area (Table 11) (as
results from a paired-samples t-test), we observe a statistically not signiﬁcant decrease from 2013 (mean = 3.17) to
2014 (mean = 3.00), t (57) = -0.77, p = 0.445 (two tailed). The mean decrease is -0.17 and the eta-squared statistic
(0.01) suggests a small effect size (Cohen, 1988).

Discussion
Overall, though, the quality of integrated reports is low. Reporters seemingly paid more attention to the form than to
the content of the reports (the average total score in 2014 for Form is 3.5 compared with 2.9 for Content). This may
be explained if, in the implementation of an IR project, the organizations have decided to concentrate their efforts
on the easier task (devising the right form to present the information) and, only later on, have they redirected their
efforts to the improvement of the content, which requires considerable resources to adapt internal processes, IT
systems, etc.
    Other main reasons that may explain the incomplete adoption of the IR framework and its low quality may be,
ﬁrst, the fact that the IR proposal, even though interesting and ambitious, today seems to be a bit idealistic and
unfeasible. In fact, the relevant costs (both explicit and implicit) sustained by project implementation do not seem
to be adequately covered by the associated beneﬁts. Some authors proposed that perfectly credible (or completely
unbiased) disclosure is not optimal because it is too costly (Core, 2001; Watts and Zimmerman, 1986). In addition,
in light of today’s general economic situation, the impact of overall costs represents a relevant issue for companies.
    Second, theoretical studies have highlighted the risks of disclosure redundancy – above all regarding information
potentially relevant for competitors – which may undermine both the ﬁrm’s speciﬁcity and the distinctive factors
that provide the foundations for the ﬁrm’s competitive advantage (Graham et al., 2005).
    Moreover, it has to be taken into consideration the potential reaction of ﬁnancial markets and stakeholders to the
communication of some negative results, as well as their perception of low reliability of the disclosed information.
Kasznik and Lev (1995) suggested that ﬁrms are penalized for disclosing bad news early.
    With reference to the context, the implementation of the IR framework implies that agents operating inside
the economic system recognize the stakeholder approach as an important component of ﬁrm strategy. If, on
the one hand, this is a desirable situation, on the other hand, it may clash with today’s dominant economic culture
still very centred on the shareholder value model and on the company’s ﬁnancial results. Moreover, the stakeholders
may not be prepared and proﬁcient enough to understand the relevance of such a wide, systematic, and
analytical disclosure.
    Finally, another aspect preventing managers from voluntarily disclosing corporate information is driven by the
so-called ‘disclosure precedent’: on the one hand, the ﬁrm has to maintain the same disclosure pattern along time,

Concluding Remarks

The objective of this paper has been to assess the quality of integrated reports. Two research questions and two
hypotheses have been proposed.
   A framework for quality assessment of integrated reports has been developed, referring to Hammond and Miles
(2014), which has been applied to evaluate: (1) how the IR Framework has been implemented by sample companies,
and (2) the changes from 2013 to 2014.
   Hypothesis 1 (HP 1: Integrated Reports issued by companies show a low quality) is supported by our research ﬁnd-
ings, while hypothesis 2 (HP 2: Over time IR quality improves but only incrementally) is only partially conﬁrmed.
   Our quantitative ﬁndings present evidence that even though a signiﬁcant improvement of the quality from 2013
to 2014 in certain areas (namely Content and Form) can be observed, the overall quality of integrated reports is still
quite limited particularly with regard to the Content and Background areas. In addition, ﬁrms seem to give more
attention to the form than to the content of reports. It is noteworthy that the most innovative and distinctive
elements of IR are those disclosed with less quality, while a higher quality characterizes aspects traditionally
disclosed by ﬁrms, such as performance, governance, and risks. Similarly to Stubbs and Higgins’ (2014) research,
our ﬁndings seem to conﬁrm that IR, being in its early adoption stage, may take more time before such an innova-
tive disclosure mechanism emerges. They also suggest that currently IR represents a ‘transition’ (Laughlin, 1991)
from sustainability reporting rather than a radical innovation driving transformation and that the lack of
comprehensive standards may be inhibiting a more widespread adoption (Stubbs and Higgins, 2014).
   Our ﬁndings are consistent also with the work of Ruiz-Lozano and Tirado-Valencia (2016, p. 252) that
highlighted how ‘despite the effort of the analyzed companies to address the guiding principles, they still have a long
way to go especially in relation to the principle of conciseness’. In addition, we found similar results to Marx and
Mohammadali-Haji (2014) who reported quality issues with regard to materiality, description of capital, and
independent and full assurance of the reports. They outlined also that the description of risks was largely generic
and that only few companies described their objectives and strategies.
   To summarize, our empirical analysis highlighted the existence of some critical aspects related to the quality of
integrated reports, showing that the whole application of the IR model is still far away. Difﬁculties in the complete
application of the IR framework have been pointed out by the IIRC itself, which issued a document giving an
account of comments received from institutional investors and companies. Communicating the ﬁrm strategy and
the feature of the value creation process represents the most relevant difﬁculty for enterprises (IIRC, 2014), and this
is also consistent with our results.
   The results of this paper are aligned with different theoretical streams. First, they are consistent with proprietary
cost theory, which suggests that managers prefer not to disclose information that may affect the ﬁrm’s competitive
position in the market, even if this would decrease the associated cost of capital (Dye, 1986). Second, as highlighted
by Nagar et al. (2003) and Berger and Hann (2003), agency issues may explain reduced voluntary disclosure because
managers desire to avoid potential attention from investors about issues that, if fully disclosed, can represent a
threat for them. Third, according to Watts and Zimmerman (1986), political costs may also explain a reluctance
in wider disclosure as managers prefer not to disclose voluntary information that regulators might use against
the company and want to avoid being subject to political attacks (Alsaeed, 2006; Camfferman and Cooke, 2002).
   To conclude, our study suggests that the methodological efforts and recommendations on IR should be directed
towards the improvement of quality, especially with regard to reliability and selectivity of data and information
provided, instead of promoting the expansion of the contents or more complex forms of disclosure. For these
reasons, in the near future we can expect that the implementation of the IR approach will continue to be limited.
   Moreover, a debate has to begin among companies, professionals, associations, and institutions on why the
majority of ﬁrms implement IR with limited quality, and on what are the positive impacts, the real beneﬁts versus
the costs, but also the risks in terms of disclosing strategic and conﬁdential information.

   This paper contributes to the research on IR in different ways: ﬁrst, it has proposed an original framework for the
quality assessment of IR, the IRS; second, it has applied the IRS to assess the quality of a sample of integrated re-
ports, thus giving a picture of the state of the art regarding the implementation of IR; and third, it has highlighted
the main critical issues in the adoption of the IR framework, with particular regard to quality aspects.
   However, our research has some limitations typical of qualitative studies, such as content analysis. Such a meth-
odology may be characterized by an unavoidable subjectivity of the evaluation process (Hammond and Miles, 2004;
Krippendorf, 1980) as well as the difﬁculty of generalizing the results beyond the sample studied. Even though the
IRS and the scoring system have been developed by referring to previous studies, ﬁrst their deﬁnition and then im-
plementation to assess the 116 integrated reports may have implied some subjectivity. Another group of researchers
could have developed a slightly different framework and scores or found slightly different ﬁndings using the same
framework and scoring system.
   Moreover, we analyzed only integrated reports available on the Getting Started section of the IR examples’
website, which included, at the time of the study, reports considered suitable to be used as benchmarks by beginner
IR preparers. For this reason, the sample may not be representative of the average ﬁrm issuing an IR. In addition,
we have focused the analysis on a speciﬁc period of time. Considering other periods or a longitudinal analysis could
be an issue for future research.
   As IR is still evolving, our research ﬁndings suggest numerous potential areas for future research. With regard to
methodology, future research could try to increase the number of companies of the analyzed sample and the time
span of analysis, and to adopt both multivariate analysis and a case study methodology to go deeper in evaluating the
quality of integrated reports.
   Moreover, as a direct extension of this research, several issues could be further investigated. How IR quality
varies across companies, industries, and time may represent relevant research questions to explore. What are the
determinants of IR quality; both internal drivers – related to ﬁrms’ speciﬁc characteristics – and external ones –
coming from the environment where the companies operate – could be another interesting aspect to examine.
   Finally, the evidence that the overall quality of IR is quite limited requires investigating if there is an optimal level
of disclosure that has to be taken into consideration in the assessment of IR quality. Since this optimal level could be
different for the ﬁrm and for stakeholders, other aspects that seem worthy of investigation are, on the one side, the
tendency towards a convergence of the ﬁrm’s and stakeholders’ perspectives and, on the other side, from the ﬁrm’s
point of view, a deeper analysis of the trade-off between the costs and beneﬁts of high disclosure. In particular, the
trade-off between more costly administrative procedures to gather information and more risk from divulgating a
ﬁrm’s idiosyncrasies, weighed against the potential beneﬁts stemming from the lower cost of capital and steadier
relationships with strategic partners and the community at large, could be another interesting research area.
